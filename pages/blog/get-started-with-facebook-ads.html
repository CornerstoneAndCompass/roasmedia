Get Started with Facebook Ads

Rich Burns | June 16, 2020

Facebook advertising can feel overwhelming when you're just starting out. The platform is powerful, but it's also complex—there are account structures to understand, campaign objectives to choose, audiences to define, and creative to develop. If you're brand new to Facebook ads, this guide will walk you through the essential fundamentals to get your first campaigns running and learning what works for your business.

Understanding Facebook Ad Account Structure

Facebook's advertising system operates on three levels: the Ad Account, Campaigns, and Ad Sets. Think of this as a hierarchy.

Ad Account: This is the top level, where you manage billing, team permissions, and overall account settings. You'll typically have one ad account per business.

Campaigns: Under your ad account, campaigns determine your objective—what you want to achieve. Are you driving traffic to your website? Collecting leads? Driving conversions? Each campaign has one objective that applies to all ads underneath it.

Ad Sets: Ad sets sit beneath campaigns and are where you define your targeting, budget, and schedule. You might have multiple ad sets within a campaign, each targeting different audiences or geographic regions.

Ads: At the lowest level are your actual ads—the creative, copy, and landing pages. You can have multiple ad variations within each ad set, which helps with testing.

This structure allows you to organize your advertising strategically. A well-organized account makes it easier to test, optimize, and understand which parts of your strategy are working.

Choose Your Campaign Objective

Your campaign objective should align with your business goal. Facebook offers several options:

  • Awareness: For building brand recognition and reaching new audiences.
  • Consideration: For driving engagement, traffic, or messaging interactions.
  • Conversion: For driving purchases, leads, or other valuable actions on your website.

For most e-commerce businesses just starting out, Conversion is the right objective because it optimizes your budget toward customers taking action. If you're building a brand or have a long consideration cycle, Awareness or Consideration might make sense first.

Audience Targeting: Start Broad, Not Narrow

A common mistake beginners make is being too restrictive with targeting. You want to reach your ideal customer, but if you make your targeting too narrow, you won't have enough data to optimize effectively.

Start with these basic audience parameters: location, age, and basic interest or behavior targeting if available. For example: women aged 25-45 interested in fitness and wellness. This gives the algorithm room to work while staying reasonably targeted.

Avoid the temptation to layer in 20 different interests or exclude every demographic you think might not convert. Let the algorithm learn by optimizing toward conversions. You can get more sophisticated with segmentation once you have data.

One advanced tactic: create a Lookalike Audience based on your existing customers. If you have an email list or past purchasers, Facebook can find similar people. This often performs better than manually-targeted audiences for beginners.

Ad Formats: Video, Image, and Carousel

Facebook offers several ad formats. Here's what to know as a beginner:

Single Image Ads: Simple and effective. One image, headline, and body copy. Good for testing and learning.

Video Ads: Often perform better than images, especially for story-driven messaging. Even simple product videos work well. Video completion rates should inform your optimization.

Carousel Ads: Multiple images or videos users can swipe through. Great for showcasing product variety or telling a sequential story.

For your first campaign, start with single image ads or video ads. These are easier to create and test. As you gather performance data, you'll learn which formats your audience prefers.

Budget and Bidding Strategy

Set a realistic daily budget for testing—$10-20 per day is reasonable when starting. You want enough budget to gather statistically meaningful data (at least 50+ conversions before making major decisions), but not so much that you're risking significant money while learning.

For bidding, let Facebook handle it using automatic bidding. The platform will optimize toward your objective (conversions, in most cases) within your budget. Once you understand how things work, you can experiment with more manual bidding strategies.

Installing and Using the Facebook Pixel

The Facebook Pixel is a tracking code you place on your website. It serves two critical functions: it tracks conversions (so you know which ads led to purchases), and it builds an audience of people who visited your site for retargeting.

Install the pixel before running conversion campaigns. You'll need to add a piece of code to your website header. Most e-commerce platforms (Shopify, WooCommerce, etc.) have easy integrations. Once installed, set up conversion tracking to measure purchases or other valuable actions.

The pixel data is what allows Facebook to optimize your campaigns and show you which ads are actually driving revenue. Without it, you're flying blind.

Creative Best Practices for Beginners

Your ad creative is what actually stops people from scrolling. Here's what matters:

Hook quickly: The first second of a video or the first visual element of an image needs to grab attention.

Lead with benefit, not features: "Save 3 hours per week" is better than "Productivity software with AI integration."

Mobile-first: Most people see your ads on mobile. Design and write copy for that context.

Clear call-to-action: "Shop Now," "Learn More," or "Get Started" should be explicit.

Test variations: Create 2-3 different versions of your ad. Maybe change the image, the headline, or the benefit you lead with. Let the data tell you what works.

Monitor Performance and Be Patient

Your first campaigns will teach you lessons. Monitor metrics like Click-Through Rate (CTR), Cost Per Result, and Return on Ad Spend (ROAS). But don't obsess over day-to-day fluctuations. Give campaigns at least 3-7 days of data before making major changes.

The learning phase is real. Facebook's algorithm needs time to optimize, especially if you're starting with a tight budget. Patience and consistent testing will reveal what works for your specific audience and offering.

"Facebook advertising rewards patient experimenters, not people who chase daily variance. Set it up right, give it data, and optimize intelligently."

Getting Started Is the First Step

You don't need to understand everything about Facebook advertising before starting. Create a campaign, set a modest budget, and let the platform start teaching you. The fundamentals—clear objective, reasonable audience, good creative, and proper tracking—are what matter most when you're beginning.

As you run campaigns and see results, you'll develop intuition about what works for your business. The complexity becomes manageable once you understand the basic structure and start gathering real data. Start simple, test methodically, and scale what works.
