Best Way to Get Started with Facebook Ads

Rich Burns | April 22, 2020

There's a difference between technically getting Facebook ads running and actually building a sustainable, profitable advertising system. Most beginners fall into the trap of launching ads too aggressively, expecting immediate results, and getting discouraged when reality doesn't match their expectations. The best way to get started with Facebook ads requires a methodical framework that prioritizes learning over revenue—at least initially.

Phase One: Start Small and Build Your Data Foundation

The biggest advantage you have as someone starting from scratch is a clean slate. There's no sunk cost fallacy making you defend underperforming campaigns. Use this advantage to your benefit.

Start with a modest budget—$10-15 per day for 7-10 days. Your goal isn't to generate immediate revenue. Your goal is to gather data about what works. Run a single campaign targeting your ideal customer with a straightforward offer. Use simple creative: either a clean product image or a product video. Focus everything on clarity rather than complexity.

This phase teaches you three things: whether conversions are happening at all, how much they cost, and what your customer's actual journey looks like. You're establishing a baseline. Some of what you'll learn will surprise you—customers may convert on different products than you expected, or your highest-converting audience might differ from your assumptions.

Phase Two: Test Systematically, Not Randomly

After your initial test period, you'll have learnings. Now comes the critical part: test methodically rather than changing everything at once.

Select one variable to test. Maybe it's creative—create three different ad variations and run them against each other. Or maybe it's audience—test one lookalike audience versus another. Run this test for 5-7 days with equal budget allocation. Let the data accumulate, then make a decision based on performance.

Resist the urge to micro-optimize based on daily fluctuations. A campaign that underperforms on Tuesday might overperform on Saturday. Give your tests time to settle. Most platforms need 50+ conversions in the learning phase before providing reliable data.

Common variables to test as you're scaling:

  • Different audience segments (age, gender, interest overlap)
  • Different creative angles (problem-solution vs. benefit-focused vs. testimonial-driven)
  • Different ad copy and headlines
  • Different landing page experiences
  • Different product offers or bundles

This testing phase is where profitable scaling is actually built. It's not glamorous, but it's where winners are made.

Phase Three: Scale What Works, Kill What Doesn't

After 2-3 weeks of testing, you'll have enough data to identify your best-performing creative, audience segments, and offers. Now it's time to scale strategically.

Take your top-performing ad variation and audience combination and increase budget by 20-30%. Monitor performance closely. If ROAS holds steady or improves, continue scaling gradually. If ROAS drops 15-20%, pause the increase and investigate why.

Simultaneously, pause your underperforming tests. If a creative variation underperformed by 30% compared to your winner, it's not worth running. If an audience segment showed 50% higher cost-per-acquisition, eliminate it. Be ruthless about pulling the plug on losing campaigns.

This phase teaches you the discipline of efficient scaling. You're not trying to make every campaign work—you're trying to identify what works best and allocate resources accordingly.

Phase Four: Build Your Pixel Data for Retargeting

As your campaigns run, the Facebook Pixel is accumulating data about people who visit your site, add products to carts, or almost complete purchases. This is valuable data for retargeting.

After 2-3 weeks of campaigns driving traffic, you should have enough pixel data to build retargeting audiences. Create campaigns targeting people who viewed products but didn't purchase. These audiences typically convert at 3-5x higher rates than cold traffic, making them highly profitable.

Retargeting becomes your "easy money" campaigns—the place where you can maintain healthy ROAS while scaling total revenue. By week four or five, you might be running both cold acquisition and warm retargeting campaigns, with retargeting driving 30-40% of your revenue from a fraction of your budget.

Phase Five: Diversify Creatives Before Scaling Aggressively

A common scaling mistake: running the same creative continuously until it stops working. By then, you've already experienced dramatic ROAS decline. Instead, continuously develop new creative variations before you need them.

While your current top-performing creative is still strong, start testing 2-3 new angles. These don't need huge budgets—$5-10 per day each to gather initial performance data. Some will perform worse than your control. Some will perform similarly. A small percentage will perform better and become your new control.

This ensures you have a pipeline of performing creative ready to deploy. When your top creative inevitably fatigues (usually after 2-4 weeks of heavy rotation), you already have validated alternatives ready to go.

Phase Six: Build Sustainable Unit Economics

As you scale, constantly monitor your unit economics. Are you actually profitable? What's your customer acquisition cost versus your customer lifetime value? Are you scaling top-line revenue at the expense of profitability?

Many brands get seduced by the simplicity of "scale what works" without asking the harder question: "Is it profitable enough to be worth scaling?" A campaign hitting 2.5x ROAS might feel successful, but if your true margins are only 10%, you're barely breaking even.

Use your first month of ads to establish what profitable looks like for your business. Then use that as your bar for scaling. Campaigns that don't meet your profitability threshold shouldn't be scaled, no matter how positive the ROAS appears on the surface.

The Framework in Practice

This six-phase framework might take 6-8 weeks to complete properly. That seems long if you're impatient for results. But compare it to brands that scale aggressively too quickly and burn through budget learning expensive lessons about audience saturation, creative fatigue, and negative unit economics. Those brands often take 6-8 months to reach the profitability level you could hit in 8 weeks with patience.

The best way to start with Facebook ads isn't fancy. It's disciplined. It's methodical. It's small budget, systematic testing, and constant learning. It's resisting the urge to scale before you have proof that scaling works. It's building pixel data and retargeting capabilities before they become critical.

"Fast growth kills more ad accounts than slow growth ever will. Start small, test rigorously, scale only what's proven, and build systematically. That's the formula."

The brands that dominate their categories started exactly where you are—confused, uncertain, and testing with small budgets. The difference between them and everyone else isn't that they got lucky. It's that they built sustainable systems before scaling aggressively. You can do the same.
